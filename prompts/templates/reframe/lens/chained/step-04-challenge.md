## WHY THIS MATTERS

You just named the root cause in Diagnose. But you might be wrong. Your diagnosis could be:
- Too narrow (you're inside your own story)
- Too fragile (it breaks when you push on it)
- Too obvious (you missed a creative reframe)

> People who have information about an individual case rarely feel the need to know the statistics of the class to which the case belongs.

Your job: attack your own diagnosis from 3 angles before moving to solutions.

---

## THE PROCESS

Run 3 challenges internally on the **diagnosis from Step 3**. Then present all 3 to the user at once. Then ask ONE question.

**Do ALL thinking in a single response.**

---

### Challenge 1: What Could Go Wrong? (Black Hat)

Put on the critic hat. Be logical, not emotional.

Ask yourself:
- If we act on this diagnosis â€” **what's the worst that happens?**
- What are the **dangers** we haven't talked about?
- Does this diagnosis fit **past experience**? Or are we ignoring something?

Rules:
- Must be **logical**, not emotional. "I don't like it" doesn't count.
- Be specific. Name the exact risk, not vague "it might not work."
- This is about **strengthening** the diagnosis by finding weak spots, not killing it.

---

### Challenge 2: What's the Base Rate? (Outside View)

Zoom out. Forget this specific user's story. Ask yourself:

1. **Identify the reference class** â€” what category does this problem belong to?
2. **Get the base rate** â€” what typically happens in that category?
3. **Adjust from the baseline** â€” is there a specific reason this case is different?

> The curriculum team estimated 2 years. The base rate for similar teams was 7-10 years with 40% failure.

---

### Challenge 3: Flip It (Restatement / Reversal)

Take the diagnosis and reframe it completely. Two techniques â€” pick whichever produces a more interesting result:

**Technique A: Restatement**
Take the diagnosis and reword it. Change the frame:
- "You have a hiring problem" â†’ "You have a retention problem"
- "Sales are down" â†’ "The wrong customers are buying"

**Technique B: Reversal**
Flip it on its head as a provocation:
- "Customers are leaving" â†’ "What if we WANTED them to leave?"
- "The team is slow" â†’ "What if speed was the enemy?"

Use the reversal for **movement** â€” not as a real suggestion, but to see where it leads.
